Pilot proposal — Year 1

The Cotting Summer Bridge

An integrated 6-week summer program pairing Cotting students with neurotypical peers in a 1:3 ratio — designed to maximize facility use, generate sustainable revenue, and build lasting community awareness in the families who need Cotting most.

01

The core idea

Cotting’s facilities, clinical staff, and therapeutic environment sit largely underutilized from late June through August. Rather than leaving that capacity idle, the Summer Bridge opens the campus to a cohort of neurotypical children who participate alongside Cotting’s enrolled students in structured activities designed by Cotting’s own clinical and educational team.

This is not a charity program or a service project. It is a genuine shared learning experience — one that is intentionally designed around the strengths of Cotting’s students, where both groups teach each other. The neurotypical peers are not helpers. They are co-participants who happen to leave with something most summer programs can’t offer: a real understanding of how to communicate across difference.

02

Why prioritize Black and Latino families

The referral networks that surface schools like Cotting run predominantly through affluent, white professional communities. Black and Latino families with children who have disabilities face the largest information gaps about available services, the highest rates of late or missed diagnosis, and the most significant barriers to OOD placement — not because their children’s needs are less severe, but because no one told them Cotting exists.

“Mom, Cotting is the school where my cousin could go for amazing services.”
What a Summer Bridge peer goes home and says — the most credible marketing Cotting could ever have
Word-of-mouth is the primary referral channel for disability services in communities where trust in institutions is low. A child who spends 6 weeks at Cotting carries that knowledge home in a way no brochure or outreach event can replicate.
Sending districts with the highest OOD rates (Everett 18.2%, Chelsea 19.1%, Lawrence 20.4%, Lynn 21.2%) also have the highest Black and Latino student populations — these are the communities Cotting’s outreach currently reaches least effectively.
The Summer Bridge inverts the referral dynamic. Rather than waiting for the system to find these families, Cotting goes directly to the community — offering something genuinely valuable (a high-quality summer program) while building the awareness that drives future placements.
Integration benefits flow both ways. Cotting’s students gain peer interaction and generalization of skills in a less structured setting. Neurotypical peers gain communication tools, empathy, and confidence that research shows persist into adulthood.
03

Program design

Activities are designed by Cotting’s clinical and educational team to center each Cotting student’s communication modality and physical strengths. Neurotypical peers are briefed and coached, not just placed. Every activity has a clear therapeutic rationale for the Cotting students and a genuine developmental benefit for the peers.

AAC communication games
Peer pairs learn to use Cotting students’ AAC devices to communicate. Fast-paced, game-based. Peers leave knowing what it’s like to build a sentence character by character — and develop genuine patience and curiosity for non-verbal communication.
Adapted art and music
Collaborative art projects designed for mixed physical abilities — large-format painting, percussion circles, clay. Structured around what each child can do, not what they can’t. No adaptations are hidden or apologized for; they’re presented as design features.
Movement and water play
Adapted PE and pool sessions using Cotting’s existing equipment. Peers participate in the same adapted formats — which often turn out to be more creative and engaging than standard PE. Physical boundaries disappear quickly in water.
Storytelling and literacy pairs
Peer reading partners, designed by Cotting’s SLPs. Each pair creates a shared story over 6 weeks using whatever communication tools work for them — voice, device, pictures, symbols. Culminates in a family presentation day.
Community field trips
2–3 fully accessible community outings per session (libraries, parks, museums, gardens). Peers learn to navigate public spaces with someone who experiences them differently. Cotting students practice community independence with natural peer support.
Family presentation day
End-of-session celebration where each pair presents their shared story or project to families. This is the moment the peer’s family sees Cotting’s environment, meets the staff, and understands the mission firsthand — without being sold to.

The mission case

Cotting’s most urgent strategic challenge is not quality — it is visibility. The families who would benefit most from Cotting’s services are the least likely to know it exists. The Summer Bridge solves this not through marketing, but through experience. It lets the mission speak for itself, through the voices of children who spent a summer there and came home changed.

Why this money exists

Under the federal School-Based Medicaid program, any service listed in a student’s IEP that is medically necessary can be billed to Medicaid — regardless of whether the student attends a public school or an approved private school like Cotting. PT, OT, speech-language pathology, nursing support, and assistive technology evaluations all qualify. Most approved private schools in Massachusetts are not billing for these services at all, or are billing at a fraction of what is recoverable.

Cotting’s sending districts include Lawrence (84% low-income), Chelsea (78%), Lynn (79%), and Lowell (71%). A majority of students in these districts qualify for MassHealth. That means a majority of Cotting’s students are likely MassHealth-eligible — and their services are billable.

The four-step recovery plan

1
Audit current IEP documentation against Medicaid billing codes

Review all IEPs for services that map to CMS billing codes: PT (CPT 97110, 97530), OT (CPT 97165–97168), SLP (CPT 92507, 92508), nursing, psychological services, AT evaluation. Quantify the reimbursable service hours per student. This audit typically takes 4–6 weeks with a billing partner.

2
Confirm MassHealth enrollment for eligible students

Work with sending district teams to verify MassHealth enrollment status for each student. Many families in high-poverty sending districts already have MassHealth coverage; those who don’t may qualify. District special ed administrators are experienced in facilitating enrollment — this is not Cotting’s burden to carry alone.

3
Partner with a school-based Medicaid billing vendor

Engage a vendor (Public Consulting Group, Conduent, Qualidigm) on a contingency-fee model — typically 8–12% of recovered revenue with no upfront cost. The vendor handles coding, claim submission, audit defense, and compliance. Cotting’s clinical staff do not change their documentation practices; the vendor recodes existing records into billable formats.

4
Reinvest recovered revenue into enrollment capacity

Each $500K recovered funds approximately 5–6 additional student placements at no net cost increase to districts. At full recovery ($2.2M), Cotting could serve 20–25 additional students annually — a 20% enrollment increase — without raising tuition or launching a capital campaign.

Why this matters for district outreach

Districts from high-poverty communities are most financially constrained by OOD tuition costs. If Cotting’s effective net tuition decreases through Medicaid co-billing — which reduces Cotting’s operating cost per student — the financial barrier for districts like Everett, Chelsea, and Lawrence drops directly. More students from these communities can access Cotting’s services.

Key insight

Medicaid recovery is not a fundraising strategy. It is a cost-recovery strategy. The federal government has already allocated these dollars for exactly these services. Cotting is simply not yet collecting what it is owed.

The efficiency-to-enrollment flywheel

Every dollar saved in overhead can fund approximately one dollar of additional student-facing service. At Cotting’s scale, a 5% operational efficiency gain — roughly $400K–$600K annually — translates directly into the ability to serve 4–6 additional students, without a tuition increase to districts and without touching any clinical program.

None of the four efficiency levers below require Cotting to change what it teaches, how it teaches it, or who teaches it. They operate in the background of the school’s operations.

Four efficiency levers

A
Transportation consortium
Save $80K–$140K/yr

Coordinate with LABBB, South Shore Collaborative, and 2–3 nearby approved schools to share bus routes serving overlapping sending districts. A student from Newton attending Cotting and a student from Newton attending LABBB are likely on near-identical routes. Pooled routing reduces per-district cost by 15–25% and removes a common barrier to placement for distant districts.

B
Shared back-office services
Save $120K–$180K/yr

Pool HR, finance, compliance, and IT functions with 1–2 peer approved schools (Perkins, Lighthouse, or similar). Each school maintains full clinical and programmatic independence. Only the back-office functions are shared — payroll processing, benefit administration, financial reporting, DESE compliance filing. Standard model used by school networks across Massachusetts.

C
Bundled AAC and AT licensing
Save $40K–$80K/yr · Generate new revenue

Negotiate district-level software and device licenses for AAC tools (Tobii Dynavox, Proloquo2Go, Snap Core First) as a service bundled into Cotting’s tuition. Districts currently procure these independently at retail rates. Cotting buys in volume, saves money on device costs, and strengthens its value proposition to districts — who no longer need to separately budget for AT.

D
Cotting Consulting expansion
Generate $200K–$350K/yr in new revenue

Cotting Consulting currently serves ~30 districts with PD, AAC coaching, and curriculum modification support. Expanding to 50+ districts using existing staff on a part-time consulting basis — no new FTEs — generates meaningful incremental revenue. Districts can fund this through IDEA Part B or Title I professional development allocations, making it budget-neutral for them.

What not to cut

Guardrail

None of these efficiency measures touch student-to-staff ratios, therapy session frequency, assistive technology provision, or clinical supervision hours. These are the inputs that define Cotting’s outcomes — they are not cost centers to be optimized. The levers above operate exclusively in administrative, procurement, and logistical functions.
